# Cadaco All-Star Baseball: Complete Rules and Reference

## Game Summary

Cadaco All-Star Baseball is a tabletop baseball simulation designed by former major leaguer Ethan Allen and first published by Cadaco-Ellis in 1941. It was the best-selling baseball board game of all time, produced for over 50 years, and has been named one of the 50 most influential American board games ever made.

The game's core mechanic is elegant and simple. Each real Major League player is represented by a circular disc divided into numbered sectors, like slices of a pie chart. These sectors are sized proportionally to the player's actual batting statistics. A power hitter like Babe Ruth has a wide home run sector; a contact hitter like Rod Carew has large singles zones and a thin strikeout sector; a pitcher like Nolan Ryan has enormous strikeout sectors and almost no extra-base hit area at all. You place a player's disc on a spinner, spin, and the result tells you what that batter did: single, double, home run, fly out, strikeout, and so on.

The game does not model individual pitching or fielding. Every at-bat is resolved through the batter's disc. This makes it simpler than competitors like Strat-O-Matic (which uses dice and models both pitchers and batters), but also makes it accessible to younger players: Cadaco was originally targeted at boys aged 9 to 12. Later editions added a Fielding K-O Dial and Strategy Disc to give the defending player more involvement and introduce elements like stolen bases, hit-and-run plays, sacrifice bunts, and variable baserunner advancement.

A typical game takes 30 to 60 minutes. Two players each draft a team from the available player discs, set their lineups, and play a full nine-inning game following standard baseball rules. It plays well solitaire, too: many fans played entire seasons alone, keeping box scores and compiling statistics across thousands of games.

## Components

The exact contents varied by edition. A typical mid-to-late-era set (roughly 1968 to 1991) included:

- 1 Baseball Diamond game board with two "At Bat" spinner assemblies and Batting Key
- 60 to 63 All-Star player discs (the number varied by year)
- 1 Strategy Disc (double-sided: A-E on one side, F-J on the other)
- 4 plastic base runner pegs (wooden pegs in pre-1967 editions)
- 1 Scoreboard insert (cardboard panel with rotating dials for Runs Per Inning and Outs, plus printed reference keys for the K-O Dial and A-E/F-J Strategy Plays)
- 1 Score Pad
- Rules (printed inside the box lid)

Player disc color coding by position:
- White center: Pitchers and Catchers
- Grey center: Infielders
- Red center: Outfielders

---

## Setup

1. Each player selects player discs to form a team. Agree on how many players each team drafts, or divide the available discs by position to ensure both teams have a full lineup (one player at each position plus a bench). A standard lineup requires nine players.

2. Each player arranges a batting order of nine players, stacking the discs face-up with the leadoff hitter on top.

3. Insert the Scoreboard panel into the game board. Set the Runs Per Inning and Outs dials to their starting positions.

4. Determine home and visiting teams. The visiting team bats first.

5. The visiting team's manager places the batting order stack under the "At Bat" spinner on their side of the board.

---

## Core Gameplay

Play follows the rules of real baseball: three outs per half-inning, nine innings per game, with extra innings if the score is tied after nine.

### Batting

1. The batting team's manager places the current batter's disc (top of the stack) under the "At Bat" spinner.

2. The batting manager spins the spinner. When it comes to rest, the number in the sector where the spinner points is the batting result.

3. Consult the Batting Key (printed on the game board between the two spinners) to determine the outcome.

4. If the result is a single, fly ball, or ground ball, the fielding manager then spins the K-O Dial to determine runner advancement and fielding outcomes (see "The Fielding K-O Dial" below).

5. After a batter's turn is complete (whether by reaching base, making an out, or completing a home run), that disc goes to the bottom of the batting order stack, face up.

## Batting Key

### Classic 14-Number Version (1941 to c. 1974)

This is the original chart used by early editions, with runner advancement rules built directly into each result:

| # | Result |
|---|--------|
| 1 | Home Run |
| 2 | Ground out. Double play if runner on first base. |
| 3 | Batter reaches first base on error |
| 4 | Fly out. All runners advance one base. |
| 5 | Triple |
| 6 | Ground out. All runners advance one base. |
| 7 | Single. Runners advance one base. |
| 8 | Fly out. Runner on third base scores; other runners hold. |
| 9 | Walk (Base on Balls) |
| 10 | Strikeout |
| 11 | Double |
| 12 | Ground out. Runners advance only if forced. |
| 13 | Single. Runners advance two bases. |
| 14 | Fly out. Runners hold their bases. |

Notes on the classic key:
- Result 2 is always a ground out; the double play is triggered only when a runner occupies first base.
- Result 3 (error) puts the batter on first. Runners advance one base (or as forced).
- Result 4 is a sacrifice-fly-style out: the batter is out, but runners tag up and advance.
- Result 8 is a deep fly: only the runner on third scores (tagging up). Runners on first and second hold.
- Result 13 is a hard-hit single: runners advance two bases instead of the usual one, so a runner on second scores and a runner on first reaches third.

### Later 14-Number Version (Used with K-O Dial, c. 1968 onward)

Later editions simplified the Batting Key and shifted runner-advancement decisions to the K-O Dial second spin. In this version, sector 3 became a fly out instead of an error, and the distinction between sectors 7/13 (and 2/6/12, and 4/8/14) was removed from the Batting Key itself since the K-O Dial now handles those details:

| # | Result |
|---|--------|
| 1 | Home Run |
| 2 | Ground out |
| 3 | Fly out |
| 4 | Fly out |
| 5 | Triple |
| 6 | Ground out |
| 7 | Single |
| 8 | Fly out |
| 9 | Walk (Base on Balls) |
| 10 | Strikeout |
| 11 | Double |
| 12 | Ground out |
| 13 | Single |
| 14 | Fly out |

Under this version, whenever the result is a single (7, 13), fly ball (3, 4, 8, 14), or ground ball (2, 6, 12), the fielding manager spins the K-O Dial to determine the full outcome.

### Simplified 8-Number Version (1975 Only)

Cadaco briefly issued discs with only 8 sectors in 1975. This version was abandoned because it was incompatible with the enormous existing library of 14-sector discs:

| # | Result |
|---|--------|
| 1 | Home Run |
| 2 | Triple |
| 3 | Double |
| 4 | Single |
| 5 | Fly Out |
| 6 | Ground Out |
| 7 | Base on Balls |
| 8 | Strikeout |

## The Fielding K-O Dial

Editions from 1968 onward included the K-O Dial to give the fielding player more involvement and to introduce variable outcomes for hits and outs.

Note: The 1968 edition used a six-letter "K-P Dial" (K, L, M, N, O, P); later editions simplified this to a five-letter "K-O Dial" (K, L, M, N, O), dropping the P results. The charts below reflect the later K-O version, which is the more commonly encountered edition. A section on the 1968 K-P differences follows.

### How It Works

1. After the batting manager spins and gets a single, fly ball, or ground ball result, the **fielding** manager spins the "At Bat" spinner on their side.

2. The fielding manager reads the letter from the K-O Dial, a ring of letters printed around the outer rim of the spinner assembly.

3. The letter is cross-referenced with the type of hit (Fly Ball, Single, or Ground Ball) on the K-O Dial Key printed on the Scoreboard panel.

4. The chart specifies the outcome for both the batter and any baserunners.

Home runs, triples, doubles, walks, and strikeouts do **not** require a K-O Dial spin; their outcomes are resolved immediately.

### K-O Dial Key: I. Fly Ball

| Letter | Batter | Runners |
|--------|--------|---------|
| K | Safe at 2B on error | Advance 2 bases |
| L | Flies out | Score from 3B; others hold bases |
| M | Flies out | Advance 1 base (from 2B and 3B) |
| N | Flies out | Hold bases |
| O | Flies out | Runner on 3B (or runner on 2B) out attempting to advance (DP); others hold bases |

Notes:
- K is the only fly ball result where the batter reaches base (on an error). The batter reaches 2B on the error, and runners advance 2 bases.
- L is a sacrifice fly: the fly is caught, the runner on 3B tags and scores, other runners hold.
- M allows runners on 2B and 3B to tag up and advance 1 base; a runner on 1B holds.
- N is a routine fly out; nobody advances.
- O creates a double play: the fly is caught, and the lead runner (on 3B, or on 2B if 3B is unoccupied) is thrown out attempting to advance after the catch. Other runners hold.

### K-O Dial Key: II. Single

| Letter | Batter | Runners |
|--------|--------|---------|
| K | Out trying for 2B | Advance 2 bases |
| L | Safe at 1B | Advance 1 base |
| M | Safe at 1B | Advance 1 base |
| N | Safe at 1B | Advance 2 bases |
| O | Safe at 2B | Runner on 2B out at plate (runner on 1B out at 3B with 2B unoccupied); others advance 2 bases |

Notes:
- K is a single where the batter is thrown out stretching for 2B, but the hit advances all runners 2 bases before the out is recorded.
- L and M are routine singles with runners advancing 1 base each.
- N is a hard-hit single: runners advance 2 bases (a runner on 2B scores, a runner on 1B reaches 3B).
- O is an extra-base single where the batter reaches 2B, but the lead runner is thrown out: a runner on 2B is out at the plate, or if 2B is unoccupied, a runner on 1B is out at 3B. Other runners advance 2 bases.

### K-O Dial Key: III. Ground Ball

| Letter | Batter | Runners |
|--------|--------|---------|
| K | Safe at 1B on error | Advance 1 base |
| L | Out at 1B | Advance 1 base if forced from base |
| M | Out at 1B | Out at 2B (DP); others advance 1 base |
| N | Out at 1B | Advance 1 base |
| O | Out at 1B | Out at 2B (DP) (out at plate with all bases occupied); others advance 1 base |

Notes:
- K is an error: the batter reaches 1B and all runners advance 1 base.
- L is a ground out where only forced runners advance.
- M is a 6-4-3 or 4-6-3 style double play: the batter is out at 1B, the lead runner is out at 2B. Other runners advance 1 base.
- N is a ground out where all runners advance 1 base (fielder throws to first, runners move up).
- O is a double play where the batter is out at 1B and the lead runner is out at 2B; with the bases loaded, the lead runner is instead out at the plate. Other runners advance 1 base.

### 1968 K-P Dial Differences

The 1968 edition used a six-letter K-P Dial instead of K-O. The K through O results were similar but with some differences, and a sixth result (P) was included:

- Fly Ball K: Batter safe on error, runners advance 3 bases (vs. 2 bases in later editions).
- Fly Ball P: Flies out; runner on 2B out rounding 3B (DP), others advance 1 base. With 1B and 3B occupied, runner scores from 3B and runner on 1B holds.
- Single P: Safe at 1B; runner on 2B out rounding 3B (runner on 1B out rounding 2B if 2B unoccupied), others advance 1 base.
- Ground Ball P: Out at 1B; runner on 2B out rounding 3B (runner on 1B out rounding 2B if 2B unoccupied), others advance 1 base.

The P results introduced "out rounding a base" situations that added realism but also complexity. Later editions dropped these in favor of the simpler five-letter system.

## Strategy Plays

The Strategy Disc adds optional tactical plays. When one or more runners are on base, the batting manager may call for a strategy play instead of a normal at-bat.

### How Strategy Plays Work

1. The batting manager announces the strategy play **before** the spin.

2. The Strategy Disc is placed on the fielding team's spinner, on top of whatever disc is there.

3. The spinner is spun. The result letter appears on the outer ring.

4. Consult the appropriate key on the Scoreboard:
   - **A-E Strategy Key** for: Single Steal (from 1B), Double Steal (1B and 3B), Hit and Run, and Squeeze Play.
   - **F-J Strategy Key** for: Single Steal (from 2B), Double Steal (1B and 2B), and Sacrifice Bunt.

5. The batter at the plate receives statistical credit for the result of the play.

---

### A-E Strategy Charts

#### Single Steal: Runner on 1B (or Runners on 1B and 3B)

Use when a runner on 1B attempts to steal 2B.

| Letter | Batter | Runners |
|--------|--------|---------|
| A | Takes pitch | Safe at 2B, hold 3B |
| B | Grounds out | Advance 1 base |
| C | Singles | Advance 2 bases |
| D | Takes pitch | Out at 2B, hold 3B |
| E | Flies out | Hold bases |

Notes: A and D are straight steal attempts with the batter taking the pitch. On A, the steal succeeds; on D, the runner is thrown out. B, C, and E resolve both the at-bat and the steal situation simultaneously. On B, the batter grounds out but the steal effectively becomes a ground-ball advance. On C, the batter singles and runners advance 2 bases (hit-and-run bonus). On E, the batter flies out and runners hold.

#### Double Steal: Runners on 1B and 3B

Use when runners on 1B and 3B attempt a double steal simultaneously.

| Letter | Batter | Runners |
|--------|--------|---------|
| A | Takes pitch | Advance 1 base |
| B | Flies out | Hold bases |
| C | Singles | Advance 2 bases |
| D | Takes pitch | Safe at 2B, out at home |
| E | Grounds out | Advance 1 base |

Notes: A is the ideal double steal: both runners advance (runner on 3B scores, runner on 1B reaches 2B). D is the classic broken double steal: the runner from 1B takes 2B, but the catcher throws through and nails the runner from 3B at the plate.

#### Hit and Run Play: Runner on 1B (or Runners on 1B and 3B)

Use when the batting manager calls a hit and run.

| Letter | Batter | Runners |
|--------|--------|---------|
| A | Singles | Advance 2 bases |
| B | Flies out | Hold bases |
| C | Misses ball | Safe at 2B, hold 3B |
| D | Ground out | Advance 1 base |
| E | Misses ball | Out at 2B, hold 3B |

Notes: A is the textbook hit and run: the batter makes contact and the running start lets everyone advance 2 bases. C and E reflect the batter swinging and missing while the runner goes: on C the runner steals safely, on E the runner is thrown out. The hit and run is a gamble between the payoff of A and the risk of B or E.

#### Squeeze Play: Runner on 3B (and Other Bases)

Use when the batting manager calls a squeeze bunt with a runner on 3B.

| Letter | Batter | Runners |
|--------|--------|---------|
| A | Grounds out | Advance 1 base |
| B | Misses ball | Out at plate, others advance 1 base |
| C | Beats out bunt | Advance 1 base |
| D | Safe at 1B | Out at plate, others advance 1 base |
| E | Pops out | Caught off 3B (DP); others hold bases |

Notes: A is a successful squeeze: the batter bunts out but the runner from 3B scores. C is even better: the batter beats out the bunt for a hit AND the runner scores. B and D are failed squeezes: the runner from 3B is thrown out at the plate, though other runners still advance 1 base. E is the worst outcome: the batter pops up the bunt and the runner on 3B is caught off the base for a double play.

### F-J Strategy Charts

#### Single Steal: Runner on 2B

Use when a runner on 2B attempts to steal 3B.

| Letter | Batter | Runners |
|--------|--------|---------|
| F | Takes pitch | Safe at 3B |
| G | Singles | Runner scores |
| H | Takes pitch | Out at 3B |
| I | Grounds out | Safe at 3B |
| J | Lines out | Caught off 2B (DP) |

Notes: F and H are straight steal attempts. On G, the batter singles and the runner (already in motion) scores from 2B. J is the worst case: the batter lines out and the runner, caught off 2B on the steal attempt, is doubled off.

#### Double Steal: Runners on 1B and 2B

Use when runners on 1B and 2B attempt a double steal simultaneously.

| Letter | Batter | Runners |
|--------|--------|---------|
| F | Takes pitch | Advance 1 base |
| G | Flies out | Hold bases |
| H | Takes pitch | Out at 3B, safe at 2B |
| I | Singles | Advance 2 bases |
| J | Grounds out | Out at 2B (DP), safe at 3B |

Notes: F is the successful double steal: both runners advance. H is a partial success: the runner from 2B is thrown out at 3B, but the runner from 1B reaches 2B. J produces a double play on the trail runner (out at 2B), but the lead runner reaches 3B safely.

#### Sacrifice Bunt: Runner on 1B

Use when the batter bunts to advance a runner from 1B.

| Letter | Batter | Runners |
|--------|--------|---------|
| F | Grounds out | Advances 1 base |
| G | Safe at 1B | Out at 2B |
| H | Beats out bunt | Advances 1 base |
| I | Pops out | Holds base |
| J | Grounds out | Out at 2B (DP) |

Notes: F is the textbook sacrifice: batter out, runner advances. H is a bunt single with the runner also advancing. G is a fielder's choice: the batter reaches but the runner is thrown out. I and J are failed sacrifices, with J being the worst: the batter grounds into a double play.

#### Sacrifice Bunt: Runner on 2B (or Runners on 1B and 2B)

Use when the batter bunts to advance a runner from 2B.

| Letter | Batter | Runners |
|--------|--------|---------|
| F | Grounds out | Advance 1 base |
| G | Safe at 1B | Out at 3B, safe at 2B |
| H | Beats out bunt | Advance 1 base |
| I | Pops out | Hold bases |
| J | Misses pitch | Caught off 2B, holds 1B |

Notes: F is the successful sacrifice. G is a fielder's choice where the lead runner is thrown out at 3B but the trail runner (if present) reaches 2B. J is unusual: the batter misses the pitch entirely and the runner on 2B is caught off the base, though a runner on 1B holds.

## Intentional Walk

The fielding team's manager may intentionally walk a batter at any time. The manager must announce "Walk Him" or "Intentional Pass" **before** the spinner is used. The batter is placed on first base, and any forced runners advance.

---

## Baserunning (General Principles)

Runners are tracked using the four plastic pegs, placed in the holes at first, second, and third base on the game board.

Under the original 14-number system (without K-O Dial), baserunner advancement is specified directly by the Batting Key result. Under the K-O Dial system, advancement for singles, fly balls, and ground balls is determined by the second spin.

Standard baseball baserunning logic applies throughout:
- Runners must advance if forced (e.g., runner on first must advance on a single or walk).
- Runners score by reaching home plate.
- On a home run, all runners and the batter score.
- On a triple, the batter reaches third; all runners score.
- On a double, the batter reaches second; runners advance per the specific rule or K-O result.
- On a walk, the batter goes to first; runners advance only if forced.

## Scoring and Record-Keeping

- The Scoreboard's rotating dials track runs per inning and outs in the current half-inning.
- The Score Pad is used for cumulative game scoring and box scores.
- Many dedicated players kept detailed box scores across hundreds or thousands of games, comparing their tabletop players' stats against real MLB stats.

---

## Substitutions

Players may substitute player discs during the game, following normal baseball substitution rules:
- A substituted player may not re-enter the game.
- Pitching substitutions are cosmetic (since pitching is not modeled), but the new pitcher's batting disc replaces the old one in the lineup.
- Pinch hitters and pinch runners follow standard baseball conventions.

---

## Winning

The team with the most runs after nine complete innings wins. If the game is tied after nine innings, play continues with extra innings until one team leads at the end of a complete inning.

---

## Quick-Play (Simplified House Rules)

Many players, especially children, played a stripped-down version:
- Use only the Batting Key; skip the K-O Dial and Strategy Disc entirely.
- Runners advance one base on a single, two on a double, three on a triple, and all runners score on a home run.
- This version plays much faster and requires less chart-referencing, at the cost of some realism.

## Edition History

| Era | Notable Changes |
|-----|----------------|
| 1941-1958 | Black-and-white discs on heavy cardboard. Die-cut center hole. Discs sit on a cardboard block under the spinner. Green backs. Original 14-number Batting Key with built-in runner advancement rules. |
| 1959-1962 | Old-time player discs printed in red. Lighter cardboard. |
| 1962-1967 | Multi-color discs with blue-grey backgrounds. League-color coding (American vs. National). Wooden pegs. |
| 1968 | Board redesigned. Plastic disc holder replaces cardboard block (no more die-cut center hole). Plastic pegs. Six-letter K-P Dial and Strategy Disc introduced. |
| c. 1970s-1988 | K-P Dial simplified to five-letter K-O Dial (P results dropped). |
| 1975 | Briefly attempted 8-category simplified discs; abandoned due to incompatibility with existing disc library. |
| 1989-1991 | Player photographs added to discs. |
| 1995 | Game comes under MLBPA license, significantly increasing production costs. |
| Mid-1990s | Annual updates discontinued due to video game competition and licensing costs. |
| 2003 | Commemorative edition released with special player packs. |
